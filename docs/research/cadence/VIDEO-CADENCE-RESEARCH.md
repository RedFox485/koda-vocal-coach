## 1. Visual Cadence / Shot Duration

### The Data on Shot Length

The average shot length (ASL) in mainstream film has dropped from **12 seconds in 1930** to roughly **2.5 seconds today** (Cornell University cinemetrics study). Action films regularly hit the 2-second mark. The earliest films had ASLs exceeding 35 seconds.

For YouTube and short-form content, the numbers are even more aggressive:

| Context | Recommended Cut Rate |
|---|---|
| Short-form (TikTok/Reels/Shorts) | Every **2-4 seconds** |
| YouTube long-form (minutes 0-3) | Frequent visual changes, **3-5 seconds** per shot |
| YouTube long-form (minutes 3-7) | Stabilize to **5-8 seconds**, more B-roll for context |
| YouTube long-form (after minute 8) | Mix calm explanation with short energy bursts |
| Tech product demo | **3-6 seconds** per shot, tighter during feature reveals |
| Conference talk / keynote | **8-15 seconds** per visual, slower for comprehension |

### Shot Length Distribution (Not Uniform)

The key insight is that **varied shot length outperforms uniform shot length**. Top creators don't cut every 3 seconds mechanically. They use a distribution:

- **40-50% of shots**: 2-4 seconds (base rhythm, keeps eyes moving)
- **25-30% of shots**: 5-8 seconds (breathing room, let a point land)
- **15-20% of shots**: 1-2 seconds (punches, emphasis, transitions)
- **5-10% of shots**: 8-15 seconds (anchor moments, demos in action, emotional beats)

The variation itself is what holds attention. A perfectly regular cut rate becomes its own monotony.

### Retention Data from YouTube

- **50-60% of all viewer drop-offs occur in the first 3 seconds.** The hook is everything.
- Videos with visual pattern interrupts every 4 seconds achieve **58% retention** vs. **41% for static talking-head** videos of the same length.
- Adding captions increases retention by **15-25%** (OpusClip data across thousands of Shorts).
- Shorts above ~75% retention have a **3x higher chance** of algorithmic promotion.

### Completion Rates by Length (Short-Form)

| Video Length | Avg. Completion Rate | Share of Views |
|---|---|---|
| Under 15 seconds | 92% | 35% |
| 16-30 seconds | 84% | 28% |
| 31-60 seconds | 68% | 22% |

Platform averages: YouTube Shorts ~73%, TikTok ~78%, Instagram Reels ~65%.

## 2. Audio Cadence / Voiceover Pacing

### Words Per Minute by Energy Level

| Content Type / Energy | WPM Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Dense technical explanation | 120-140 | Slow for comprehension of complex material |
| E-learning / tutorial | 140-160 | Balance of clarity and engagement |
| Standard narration | 150-160 | The workhorse range for most video content |
| Story-driven / conversational | 150-170 | Natural energy, "Talk Like TED" ideal range |
| High-energy commercial | 160-180 | Urgency, excitement, calls to action |
| Maximum sustainable pace | 170+ | Not recommended for more than 5 minutes (listening fatigue) |

For comparison: average adult reading speed is 250-300 WPM. Video narration is inherently lower-density than text.

### The Science of Pauses

Pauses function as punctuation in audio. Research from public speaking and neuroscience:

| Pause Duration | Function | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **0.3-0.5 seconds** | Comma-equivalent, separates minor thoughts | Between clauses, natural breathing |
| **0.5-1 second** | Period-equivalent, separates complete ideas | Between sentences, after a data point |
| **1-2 seconds** | Paragraph break, signals topic shift | Before introducing a new concept |
| **2-3 seconds** | Power Pause, creates emphasis and lets a key point sink in | After your single most important statement |
| **3+ seconds** | Dramatic silence, use sparingly | Before a reveal, after asking a rhetorical question |

**Key finding:** Speakers who don't pause negatively affect listener comprehension (Toastmasters research). Points preceded by pauses are remembered longer. A 3-second pause feels like 10 seconds to the speaker but lands perfectly for the audience.

**Overuse warning:** Silence only stands out when it contrasts with speech. If every sentence gets a power pause, none of them land.

### Vocal Variation Beyond Speed

- **Pitch shifts** signal new information vs. supporting detail
- **Volume drops** before key reveals create a "lean in" effect
- **Rhythm breaks** (suddenly slowing after a fast section) function as audio pattern interrupts
- Listeners retain **40% more content** when narration includes natural pacing and tonal cues vs. flat delivery (voiceover industry research)

## 3. Information Density and Cognitive Load

### Mayer's Multimedia Learning Principles (The Canon)

Richard Mayer's research is the gold standard. The principles most relevant to video production:

| Principle | Rule | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Coherence** | Exclude extraneous material | Cut "interesting but irrelevant" content. Audiences often remember seductive details better than the core message -- and that's bad. |
| **Signaling** | Highlight essential information | Use visual cues (arrows, highlights, zoom) to direct attention to what matters. Frees up cognitive capacity. |
| **Redundancy** | Graphics + narration > Graphics + narration + on-screen text | Do NOT put your full script on screen while narrating. Narrate OR show text, not both simultaneously. On-screen text competes with visuals for the visual channel. |
| **Temporal Contiguity** | Present words and pictures simultaneously | Don't show a feature, then explain it 10 seconds later. Show and tell at the same time. |
| **Spatial Contiguity** | Place related text near related graphics | Labels should be on the thing, not in a legend at the bottom. |
| **Segmenting** | Break content into learner-paced segments | In video: use clear section breaks, chapter markers, visual transitions between topics. |
| **Modality** | Graphics + narration > Graphics + text | Narrate over visuals rather than showing text over visuals. Uses both channels efficiently. |

### Dual-Channel Processing

Humans have two processing channels: visual (pictorial) and auditory (verbal). Each has limited capacity. The optimal video uses both channels without overloading either:

- **Visual channel:** UI demo, animation, diagrams, screen recording
- **Auditory channel:** Narration, sound effects, music

**Overload pattern (bad):** Narrating while showing dense text on screen. Both compete for the same channels.
**Optimal pattern (good):** Narrating while showing the product in action. Each channel carries complementary information.

### How Much Information Per Minute?

- A 5-minute video script fits on less than one page of text.
- A 20-minute video lecture contains roughly the same raw data as a 5-minute read.
- **Practical rule for technical demos:** Introduce no more than **1 new concept per 30-45 seconds** in a fast-paced video, or **1 per 60-90 seconds** when the concepts are complex.
- Educational videos under 6 minutes show nearly complete viewership; beyond 9-12 minutes, engagement drops significantly.

### The "Show Don't Tell" Ratio for Technical Content

From conference talk best practices and Apple keynote analysis:

- **One idea per slide/visual**
- Code snippets over 5 lines belong in a live demo, not on a slide
- Use diagrams over text for architecture
- **Ideal ratio for a 2-3 minute product demo: 70% show (product in action), 30% tell (narration/context)**
- For conference talks: flip the visual before explaining it. Let the audience's curiosity activate before you narrate.

## 4. Energy Curves

### The Fundamental Pattern

Hollywood intensity graphs and engagement curve research converge on the same shape:

```
Energy
  ^
  |     /\        /\          /\
  |    /  \      /  \        /  \     /
  |   /    \    /    \      /    \   /
  |  /      \  /      \    /      \ /
  | /        \/        \  /        V
  |/                    \/
  +---------------------------------> Time
  Hook   Build  Valley  Build  Valley  Climax  Resolve
```

**Critical rules:**
1. **Each peak should be higher than the previous peak.** Escalating intensity.
2. **Each valley should be higher than the previous valley.** If a valley drops below the previous one, you lose the audience.
3. **Valleys are not dead time.** They let viewers process, breathe, and prepare for the next rise. They must still advance the narrative.
4. **Don't blow your biggest moment early.** If you peak at 20% of the way through, you have nowhere to go.

### Adapted for a 2-3 Minute Demo Video

For a 2:30 video (150 seconds):

| Timestamp | Energy Level | Content |
|---|---|---|
| **0:00-0:05** | HIGH (8/10) | Cold open hook. The "wow" moment or the problem stated viscerally. |
| **0:05-0:15** | Medium (5/10) | Context. Who this is for, what the problem is. Brief. |
| **0:15-0:30** | Rising (6/10) | First feature reveal. Show it working. |
| **0:30-0:45** | HIGH (8/10) | The "aha" moment. The thing that makes this different. |
| **0:45-1:00** | Dip (5/10) | Briefly explain how it works. Breathing room. |
| **1:00-1:30** | Building (6-7/10) | Second major feature or deeper dive. Show capability. |
| **1:30-1:50** | PEAK (9/10) | The climax demo. Full system in action. Most impressive moment. |
| **1:50-2:10** | Settling (6/10) | Results, outcomes, what this means. |
| **2:10-2:30** | Resolving (7/10) | CTA or final impression. End on confidence, not a whimper. |

### The "End Higher Than You Started" Rule

The last 10% of your video should have more energy than the first 10% (after the hook). The hook is a spike to grab attention; the ending is the emotional takeaway. If you end flat, that's what they remember.

## 5. Pattern Interrupts

### What They Are

A pattern interrupt is any unexpected change in visual, audio, or information flow that re-engages the viewer's reticular activating system (the brain's attention gatekeeper). It exploits a survival mechanism: our brains are wired to notice change.

### The Data

- Videos with pattern interrupts in the first 3 seconds see engagement rates **up to 3x higher** than those without.
- Pattern-based attention hooks increase completion rates by **41%** (Inceptly data).
- **71% of viewers** decide within 3 seconds whether to keep watching; the average decision happens in **1.7 seconds**.

### Recommended Frequency

| Content Type | Pattern Interrupt Interval |
|---|---|
| Short-form (under 60s) | Every **3-5 seconds** |
| Product demo (2-3 min) | Every **7-15 seconds** |
| Long-form YouTube | Every **15-30 seconds** |
| Conference keynote | Every **9-10 minutes** (Jobs rule) |

### Types of Pattern Interrupts (Ranked by Production Effort)

**Low effort (editing-only):**
1. **Jump cut** -- cut out dead air, skip forward in the same shot
2. **Zoom punch** -- quick 10-20% zoom in/out on the same footage
3. **Text overlay** -- keyword or stat appears on screen
4. **Sound effect** -- whoosh, click, pop on transitions
5. **Caption style change** -- bold a word, change color

**Medium effort:**
6. **B-roll cut** -- switch to supplementary footage (screen recording, close-up, diagram)
7. **Camera angle change** -- switch between angles every 15-20 seconds
8. **Split screen** -- show two things simultaneously
9. **Motion graphics** -- animated callout, arrow, highlight

**High effort:**
10. **Location/background change** -- different setting entirely
11. **New speaker** -- bring in a different voice or face
12. **Live demo switch** -- transition from slides/narration to live product interaction

### The "Novelty Budget"

Not all interrupts are equal. Each one spends a little of the viewer's cognitive capacity. Too many = overstimulating and exhausting. Too few = boring. The rule of thumb:

- **Short-form**: High novelty budget. Burn through variety fast.
- **Technical/educational**: Lower novelty budget. Each interrupt should serve comprehension (highlight, zoom to detail, diagram), not just flash.
- **Never interrupt during a key concept landing.** Let the important thing breathe for 5-8 seconds before the next visual change.

## 6. Demo-Specific Cadence

### What Makes Tech Demos Boring

1. **Narrating the UI literally.** "And then I click here, and then I click here..." Nobody cares about your clicks. They care about what happens.
2. **Feature list structure.** Walking through features 1-2-3-4-5 at uniform energy is a death march.
3. **No stakes.** If you don't establish why this matters before showing it, the demo is just movement on a screen.
4. **Too much setup.** If you spend 45 seconds of a 2-minute video explaining context, you've lost them.
5. **Static screen recordings.** Unedited screen recordings with no zoom, no highlights, no cuts.

### What Makes Tech Demos Engaging

**Apple Keynote Techniques:**
- **The 9-Minute Rule (Steve Jobs):** Switch modes (story to demo to story to demo) every 9-10 minutes. Neuroscientists confirm the brain's "attention stopwatch" resets around this interval. For our 2-3 minute videos, the equivalent is switching modes every **30-45 seconds**.
- **Tease before reveal.** Don't open with the feature. Open with the problem or the desire. Then reveal.
- **"One more thing..."** Hold back your best feature. The audience thinks the demo is winding down, then you escalate.
- **Language:** Never use jargon in the reveal moment. Jobs said "magical" and "incredible", not "leveraging our proprietary framework."
- **Tight rehearsal.** Every Apple demo is rehearsed to perfection. The spontaneity is planned. Jobs used a 6-step rehearsal process for every presentation.

**Viral Product Demo Patterns:**
- **Open with the output, not the input.** Show the result first, then show how you got there. Creates curiosity.
- **The "before/after" frame.** Showing the painful old way for 5-10 seconds, then the smooth new way, creates instant contrast.
- **Speed runs.** Show the full workflow at 2x speed with music, then slow down to highlight key moments. Demonstrates capability without boring the audience.
- **Real-world context.** Show the product solving a real problem in a real setting, not in a vacuum.

**Retention Editing for Demos:**
- Avoid the spike-and-drop pattern (exciting intro followed by a cliff). Bridge the intro's energy into the main content smoothly.
- Use B-roll and split-screen to keep visuals fresh during narration.
- Sound design is often overlooked but plays a major role: UI sounds, transition sounds, subtle feedback sounds make demos feel polished and immersive.
- Subtitles improve watch time significantly, especially since many users watch without sound.

### The "Show the Magic" Framework

For any product demo, structure around these three beats:
1. **The Problem** (10-15% of runtime): Make it felt, not just stated.
2. **The Magic** (60-70% of runtime): The product solving the problem. Multiple angles, escalating capability.
3. **The Implication** (15-20% of runtime): What this means for the user. The "so what."

## 7. Music and Ambient Sound

### When to Use Background Music

| Scenario | Music? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Montage / speed-run sequences | Yes, driving | Music carries energy when narration drops out |
| Emotional storytelling sections | Yes, subtle | Sets mood, adds production value |
| Title cards and transitions | Yes, brief hits | Punctuates structure |
| Intro/outro | Yes | Establishes brand feel |
| Screen recording with narration | Subtle or none | Music can distract from comprehension |
| Dense technical explanation | No or very quiet | Cognitive load is already high |
| Feature reveal "wow" moment | Music swell, then drop | The drop to silence after a swell amplifies impact |

### When Silence is More Powerful

- **Right before a key reveal.** Drop the music 1-2 seconds before the moment. The silence creates anticipation.
- **After a powerful statement.** Let it hang in dead air for 2-3 seconds.
- **During complex UI interaction.** If the viewer needs to follow what's happening on screen, remove competing audio.
- **Product-only demos** (carousel ads, mobile-first explainers, visual storytelling with text overlays): Music optional; silence can be your greatest filter, removing clutter and letting graphics speak.

### Music Selection Rules

1. **No lyrics when there's voiceover.** Competing vocals destroy comprehension.
2. **Match the BPM to your cut rate.** If you're cutting every 3-4 seconds, music around 120-140 BPM feels synchronized. Slower cuts need slower music.
3. **Use the music's structure.** Build sections for building energy, drops for reveals, quiet sections for explanations. Don't fight the music's own arc.
4. **Treat music as emotional architecture.** It tells the audience how to feel about your pacing. Upbeat = excitement. Ambient = focus. Driving = urgency.
5. **Volume levels:** Background music should sit at **-18 to -24 dB** below narration. If a viewer has to strain to hear the voiceover, the music is too loud.

### Sound Design Micro-Techniques

- **UI click sounds** on button presses in screen recordings (subtle, not cartoonish)
- **Whoosh** on transitions between sections
- **Rising tone** before a reveal
- **Confirmation chime** when showing a successful result
- **Ambient room tone** under voiceover prevents the "studio vacuum" feeling

## 8. Actionable Rules for Our Pipeline

These are the concrete, applicable rules distilled from all the research above. Reference these during production.

### Pre-Production

1. **Script to the energy curve first.** Map your 2:30 video to the timestamp/energy table in Section 4 before writing a single word.
2. **One concept per 30-45 seconds.** If you have 5 features, you need 2:30-3:45 of runtime minimum, or you need to cut features.
3. **Write the hook first, write it last, rewrite it again.** 50-60% of all drop-offs happen in 3 seconds.
4. **Script at 140-160 WPM** for technical content. A 2:30 video = roughly 350-400 words of narration.
5. **Plan your pattern interrupts.** Mark them in the script every 7-15 seconds. At least one per concept.

### Production

6. **Record at least 2 camera angles** or plan for B-roll. You need visual variety every 3-6 seconds.
7. **Capture clean audio.** Bad audio kills retention faster than bad video.
8. **Record demos at normal speed.** You can speed them up in post; you can't slow them down without artifacts.
9. **Over-record.** Shoot 3x what you need. Editing is where the cadence is built.

### Editing

10. **Target ASL (average shot length) of 3-5 seconds** for demo content.
11. **Never hold a static shot for more than 8 seconds** unless it's an intentional anchor moment.
12. **Apply the Mayer Redundancy Principle:** Narrate over visuals. Don't put your narration script on screen as text simultaneously.
13. **Use zoom punches and highlights** on screen recordings to direct attention (Mayer's Signaling Principle).
14. **Cut dead air ruthlessly.** Jump cuts are expected and appreciated in modern content.
15. **Add captions.** 15-25% retention improvement. Non-negotiable.

### Audio

16. **Narration: 140-160 WPM** for the base, dropping to 120 WPM for complex explanations, pushing to 170 WPM for exciting moments.
17. **Use power pauses (2-3 seconds)** after your single most important statement. Use shorter pauses (0.5-1 second) between regular ideas.
18. **Background music at -18 to -24 dB** below voice. No lyrics.
19. **Drop music to silence** 1-2 seconds before your biggest reveal.
20. **Add subtle sound design:** transition whooshes, UI clicks, confirmation sounds.

### Structure

21. **Open with the output/result**, not the setup. Show the magic, then explain it.
22. **Alternate modes every 30-45 seconds:** narration to demo to results to narration.
23. **Each valley must be higher than the previous valley.** Momentum only goes up.
24. **End at 7/10 energy, not 4/10.** The last impression is the lasting impression.
25. **The before/after frame** is your most powerful structural tool for demos. Pain, then solution.
